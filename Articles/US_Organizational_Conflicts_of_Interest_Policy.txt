U.S. Organizational Conflicts of Interest Policy

COMPANY must comply with U.S. Government requirements at the state, local, and federal levels to provide impartial and unbiased services, and to mitigate and disclose any actual or potential organizational conflicts of interest (OCIs).

OCIs can arise as a result of COMPANY's client work in the public or private sector. To assess and address potential OCIs, U.S. Government opportunities and private sector opportunities with a U.S. Government nexus should be flagged for evaluation of potential or actual OCIs.

This policy applies to all proposed work relating to U.S. Government contracts, U.S. Government-funded opportunities, and work assisting clients on U.S. regulatory strategy, whether performed for a public or private entity. U.S. Government includes state, local, and federal government.

An OCI may exist if an organization has interests that could be perceived as biasing, or would in fact bias, the advice or assistance provided to government clients. OCIs may also arise if an organization has interests that could provide unfair competitive advantages through access to certain types of information not available to all potential competitors.

At the U.S. federal level, there are three main types of OCIs:

- Impaired objectivity conflicts can arise when COMPANY's business interests may inappropriately influence COMPANY USG's ability to render impartial assistance or advice to the government. This may occur, for example, when COMPANY USG is asked to evaluate goods or services provided by itself, an affiliate, or a client that COMPANY serves on the same goods or services.
- Biased ground rules conflicts can arise if COMPANY USG helps establish the terms of a procurement by drafting statements of work, cost estimates, technical specifications, or evaluation or source selection criteria, and then competes directly or indirectly for that same work, or helps clients compete for the same work.
- Unequal access to information conflicts can arise when COMPANY USG obtains access to non-public information while performing one contract that could provide an unfair competitive advantage in a future government procurement. This type of conflict could also arise if COMPANY USG or its affiliates retain or hire former government employees who had access to relevant, competitively sensitive, non-public information during their government service, including classified information, source selection data, and proprietary information not available to the public.

There may be other types of conflicts. Individual contracts or specific federal or state agencies may have additional requirements or other conflicts, such as personal conflicts or financial conflicts, that should be evaluated as part of the conflict review process.

Before submitting a proposal for a U.S. Government contract or task order, the Ethics & Compliance Conflicts team will assess OCIs in accordance with applicable legal and contractual requirements. The review process includes neutral stakeholders to help safeguard objectivity.

The standard OCI review process includes:

- Gathering information on COMPANY client work in the public and private sectors to evaluate whether a potential or actual OCI exists and whether a future OCI is likely.
- Assessing conflicts of interest and, as appropriate, developing a proposed plan to address any actual or potential OCI.
- Providing guidance to the client service team on the appropriate approach to address any potential or actual OCIs.

A proposed plan to address an actual or potential OCI may include:

- Disclosure and mitigation steps.
- Declining or excluding certain work.
- In rare circumstances, agreeing to limited restrictions on future client service.

Private sector client service teams that are developing or seeking to engage in client service opportunities with a U.S. Government nexus must register relevant studies in Engage. Examples include support on a proposal or application to the U.S. Government, or strategies for sales to the U.S. Government. The Conflicts team will review these matters for OCIs using the same process applied to public sector client opportunities.

The Accountable Senior Partner and Responsible Partner must ensure that an engagement complies with this policy. This includes ensuring that the client service team complies with any applicable OCI mitigation or avoidance plan, whether for COMPANY USG or an affiliate.

All colleagues must follow this policy and all other applicable Firm conflict of interest policies.

Because PSSS work can involve a range of risks, DCSs, EDs, and colleagues involved in proposal development should seek advice from cell leaders and raise questions or concerns to relevant Firm functions, such as Conflicts, Risk, Legal, Finance, Communications, or the CSRC, as early as possible.

Partners, cell leaders, and Firm function colleagues can bring queries or concerns to the CSRC or other committees. Questions about interpreting this policy, including how it relates to other Firm policies and guidelines, should be directed to Firm Risk, the Conflicts team, or Firm Legal.

Firm members must comply with this policy, act with integrity, and report violations. If local law imposes stricter requirements, colleagues must follow the law.

The Firm does not tolerate retaliation for reporting in good faith or participating in a review or investigation. Nothing in this policy prohibits reporting possible violations of laws or regulations to a governmental agency, regulatory body, or local authority.

Failure to comply with this policy may result in disciplinary action, including separation from the Firm, consistent with the Standard on Accountability. Consequences are applied fairly and consistently across all roles and geographies.

For third parties engaged by the Firm, violations may result in consequences up to and including termination of arrangements with the Firm.
