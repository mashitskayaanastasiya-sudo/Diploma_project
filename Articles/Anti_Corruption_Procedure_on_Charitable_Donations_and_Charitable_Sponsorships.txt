Anti-Corruption Procedure on Charitable Donations and Charitable Sponsorships

This procedure supplements the Anti-Corruption Policy. It describes the process Firm members must follow so the Firm makes charitable donations and charitable sponsorships appropriately and fairly.

Charitable donations and charitable sponsorships must be used for their intended purpose and must not be construed as bribes or improper payments.

The procedure does not cover pro bono engagements or zero-fee engagements with government entities, which are addressed through other Firm policies and standards.

Charitable donations and sponsorships must be executed within a controlled framework that:

- Mitigates risk.
- Ensures fairness in considering beneficiaries.
- Complies with applicable laws.
- Reflects Firm values.

All charitable donations and charitable sponsorships made on behalf of the Firm must:

- Be made to a charitable organization with a track record of commitment to its mission or other proof of legitimacy.
- Avoid exposing the Firm to reputational damage.
- Not be made in cash, cash equivalents, buyer cards, or purchasing cards.
- Be accompanied by a Firm cover letter confirming the legitimate purpose of the donation.
- Be documented and recorded accurately.
- Be properly receipted.
- Comply with laws, regulations, the Code, and Firm policies.
- Be charged to the commodity code for gifts, awards, and charity.
- Not be charged to a client WIP or client charge code.
- Not be made to a prohibited recipient.
- Not be made for a prohibited purpose.

Before committing the Firm to a charitable donation or sponsorship, the Firm member must confirm that the organization:

- Meets the definition of a charitable organization.
- Has a social impact mission.
- Is duly registered under local law.
- Will not expose the Firm to potential or actual reputational damage.

The Firm member must request pre-clearance through the charitable giving anti-corruption pre-clearance process. The request requires certification that the donation or sponsorship is not for a prohibited recipient or prohibited purpose.

If the donation presents diligence red flags or is recommended by a government official, the case is escalated to Compliance.

If the pre-clearance request is rejected, the Firm member may not further explore the donation or sponsorship.

If pre-clearance is granted, the Firm member may proceed with the remaining required steps.

Final pre-approval from the location managing partner is required when:

- The donation exceeds US $1,000.
- The donation is requested by a government official.
- An owner, board member, or executive of the proposed recipient is a client, Firm employee, government official, or close relative of such a person.

If the location managing partner requested the donation, the office managing partner must provide final pre-approval.

Firm members must also follow local office and practice approval requirements and work with local reach and engagement colleagues for use of name, logo, or marketing issues.

Where relevant, Firm members must obtain pre-approvals required under the Outside Activities Policy and Standard on Board Memberships.

Once final approval is obtained, the Firm member should submit the invoice through the standard process and include the pre-clearance confirmation.

Pre-clearance applies only to a specific donation request. Multiple donation requests to the same organization require separate pre-clearance each time.

If the charitable organization is new to the Firm, onboarding through the supplier portal may be required.

After the donation is made, the completed charitable donation or sponsorship cover letter must be sent to the charitable organization. The organization must complete the acknowledgement and send it to Compliance.

Additional approval rules apply for donations to public sector institutions or agencies. Anti-Corruption Compliance, with Legal as appropriate, reviews donations to United States and non-United States public sector institutions, agencies, schools, libraries, and public-sector-owned or operated businesses.

Firm members must consult Anti-Corruption Compliance before agreeing to donate to a charitable organization, school, or public sector institution in connection with a public sector sales promotion, tender, or bid.

Anti-Corruption Compliance must approve charitable donations if a government official has or may have a direct or indirect interest in the organization.

North America Firm members should use the North America charitable contributions process for charitable donations, charitable sponsorships, and membership-related expenses.

For volunteering activities as a Firm member or on Volunteer Time Off, colleagues should contact the Global Social Responsibility team. If anti-corruption gating questions are answered affirmatively, the case must be escalated to Anti-Corruption Compliance.

Firm members must remain alert for corruption red flags throughout the donation lifecycle. A red flag may include a donation requested by a government official who is a decision maker for an open RFP, letter of proposal, permit application, or similar matter.

Firm members must immediately report suspected improper charitable donations or sponsorships, recipients making improper payments to government officials, or violations of this procedure or the Anti-Corruption Policy.

Natural disaster donations of goods may be made without Anti-Corruption Compliance pre-approval to Red Cross, Red Crescent, government entities addressing the disaster, or directly to affected individuals. The responsible colleague must inform the Anti-Corruption team within one week and ensure compliance with international trade controls.

Other exception requests must be reviewed and approved in advance by Legal and Anti-Corruption Compliance.

Memberships in entities that will represent the Firm in interactions with government entities or government officials must follow the Anti-Corruption Compliance due diligence process.

Definitions:

- Charitable donation means monetary or in-kind donations to a charitable organization.
- Charitable donations include in-kind contributions, monetary donations, grants, charitable event sponsorships, fellowships, and gala tables.
- Charitable donation does not include payments made in exchange for a benefit, memberships, event participation, marketing rights, or the Firm matching program.
- Charitable organization means a tax-exempt organization under Section 501(c)(3) in the United States or an equivalent outside the United States, or a certified academic institution.
- Charitable sponsorship means financial support, products, services, or anything of value provided to a third party in exchange for rights allowing the Firm to associate its brand with another brand in a broad, publicly visible way.

Prohibited recipients include:

- Individuals.
- Organizations whose primary purpose is political promotion, influencing legislation, or supporting candidates for public office.
- Organizations in restricted jurisdictions.
- Sanctioned parties.
- Non-profits without required exempt status.

Prohibited purposes include:

- Being or appearing to be a bribe, kickback, or other wrongful conduct.
- Procuring future Firm business.
- Improperly obtaining or retaining business or official government action.
- Securing an improper advantage.
- Inducing anyone to act unfairly.
- Circumventing the gifts, meals, hospitality, and travel procedure.
- Causing the Firm to be involved in sanctionable activities.

Non-compliance may result in disciplinary action, including separation from the Firm.
