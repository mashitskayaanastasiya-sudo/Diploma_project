Recruiting and Hiring Policy

COMPANY is committed to a merit-based culture and to fostering and preserving inclusivity across the Firm. The hiring process must be free from bias and undue influence from referrals and recruitment activities through interviews and hiring decisions. All employment decisions must be independent, based on merit, and free from improper influence.

The Firm strives to maintain a meritocratic work environment that can attract, develop, and retain exceptional people. This policy aligns with the Code of Conduct and applies globally to all hiring efforts, including recruiting, referring, and interviewing for all roles and internships on temporary, at-will, fixed, regular, or permanent terms.

Firm members must also comply with any additional requirements in their jurisdiction and should seek guidance from People Leaders, Ethics & Compliance, or COMPANY Legal when questions arise.

The hiring process must comply with relevant laws and regulations and reflect the Firm's ethical standards. Candidates must be assessed based on qualifications, skills, and potential to contribute to the Firm's success.

The hiring process must avoid bias, undue influence, interference, favoritism, coercion, and unfair advantage. The Firm will not make ad hoc hires, including unpaid internships, or develop ad hoc programs to hire specific individuals.

Anticompetitive agreements with competitors for talent are prohibited and can create criminal, civil, and reputational consequences.

The Firm's rule about hiring is simple: employment decisions must be based on merit and not made to improperly influence anyone.

Colleagues involved in hiring decisions, and third parties acting on behalf of the Firm, may not offer employment or internships:

- In exchange for, as a reward for, or as an inducement to obtain work.
- To unduly influence a decision by a Government Official.
- To otherwise obtain an improper advantage for COMPANY or themselves.

Government Officials include:

- Employees or elected or appointed officials of local, state, provincial, regional, or national governments.
- Anyone acting in an official capacity on behalf of a government.
- Political parties, party officials, and candidates for political office.
- Officials or employees of public international organizations such as the World Bank or United Nations.
- Employees of state-owned or state-linked enterprises that are owned 50 percent or more by a government or subject to public procurement rules.

Personnel involved in hiring must ensure that position requirements are clearly articulated, that the role genuinely needs to be filled, and that the candidate has the required education and experience.

Colleagues may not participate in any aspect of the hiring process where they have an existing personal or business relationship with the applicant or applicant's relative, or where they referred the candidate. Participation includes interviewing, decision-making, coaching the candidate, or attempting to influence the outcome.

If asked to participate in a process where a conflict may exist, Firm members must tell the recruiter or process manager and recuse themselves.

The Firm recognizes that a job or internship could be considered a bribe if offered to a child, family member, or friend of a client or Government Official in exchange for a business opportunity or favorable treatment.

There are no must-hires in recruiting. The Firm does not lower standards for anyone, including referrals from Firm members, clients, potential clients, or others. Referrals may be submitted for standard resume screening through Talent Attraction's Global Referral Program, but interviews are not guaranteed.

All candidates must successfully complete background screening before starting work with COMPANY, subject to local law. HR manages this process.

Hiring current or former Government Officials, or people with a close relationship to a Government Official, creates elevated risk and may require additional legal and ethics review. Laws may prohibit hiring certain Government Officials for a period of time or restrict the work they may perform if hired.

Only HR or Talent Attraction may negotiate employment with a Government Official, a person in a close relationship with a Government Official, or a relative of a Government Official.

If a Government Official or related person approaches a Firm member about employment with COMPANY, the Firm member must refer them to Talent Attraction, which will manage communication and documentation.

Applicants who are current or former Government Officials must disclose whether they need consent from their government employer before accepting a position with COMPANY.

If a Firm member refers a candidate who is a Government Official, was received from a Government Official, or has a close relationship with a Government Official, the Firm member must disclose this to Talent Attraction when making the referral.

For Government Officials, the Firm generally will not extend a job offer within one year after the applicant served in specified procurement, source selection, program management, contracting, payment, rate-setting, or claims roles involving COMPANY.

Hiring of Government Officials must be managed through Talent Attraction. Deviations from the one-year cooling-off period require HR and Talent Attraction to consult Firm Legal before proceeding.

Familial relationships between colleagues can create workplace risks, including the appearance of favoritism in hiring, evaluations, assignments, supervision, or career development. Firm members may not have a familial relationship with a colleague where either person has actual or potential influence over the other's work or career trajectory.

The Firm may choose not to hire relatives of current colleagues, including Partners and senior non-Partners, to avoid actual or potential conflicts.

Considering or employing a relative of a current Firm member requires review to ensure sufficient separation between the people and roles involved, while following merit-based recruiting and interviews. Relevant factors include roles, locations, offices, sectors, cells, and functions.

Approval is required from the relevant Regional People Leader together with the Global Head of Human Resources and Chief People Officer.

Once an application is submitted, the involved Firm member must promptly report the relationship to Talent Attraction. Talent Attraction will work with HR or Partner Personnel to conduct a risk assessment and determine whether the process may continue.

If the relative is hired, Talent Attraction will work with HR or Partner Personnel to create a Memo of Understanding outlining requirements to be observed.

Partners, supervisors, Engagement Managers, Associate Partners, equivalent roles, or managers who become aware of familial relationships covered by this policy must report them to the appropriate HR or PD leader.

When the Firm competes for talent, recruiting decisions must be made independently. The Antitrust Policy prohibits agreements with competitors for talent not to solicit or hire each other's employees.

Recruiting from client organizations is guided by two goals:

- Attracting top talent to meet the Firm's evolving needs.
- Preserving client relationships.

This policy applies to recruiting individuals for permanent, internship, and temporary roles across client organizations, including alumni employed by clients and contractors at client organizations.

Recruiting from clients is permissible, including client candidates who apply to the Firm and candidates the Firm sources. Appropriate DCS involvement is required at various stages depending on the recruitment activity.

Firm members must not disclose details about client relationships to a prospect or candidate or mention that a policy exists for certain organizations, unless the DCS approves an exception.

Firm members must comply with this policy, act with integrity, and report violations. Local law controls where it imposes stricter requirements.

The Firm does not tolerate retaliation for good-faith reporting or participation in a review or investigation.

Failure to comply may result in disciplinary action, including separation from the Firm. Third-party violations may result in consequences up to and including termination of arrangements with the Firm.
