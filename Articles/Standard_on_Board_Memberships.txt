Standard on Board Memberships

This standard supplements and is enforceable under the Outside Activities Policy.

Firm members may not hold membership on the board of a for-profit organization, hospital, insurer, trade association, or public sector entity except in limited approved circumstances.

Private sector board membership may be permitted only in these limited cases:

- For Senior Partners, when the board membership is part of a retirement program and approved by the Senior Partner Committee chair, the General Counsel, and the Chief Risk Officer. Pre-retirement board memberships for Senior Partners normally will not be approved unless full retirement is scheduled within 12 months.
- For COMPANY Investment Office employees, when the board membership is connected to an investment made or being made by MIO, approved in advance by the MIO board or delegated subcommittee, and evaluated under criteria developed with Firm Legal and the Client Service Risk Committee.
- Where the Firm has made a material investment in a privately held company, no reasonable alternative exists to protect the Firm's investment, the need for protection outweighs the risks, and approval is obtained from Firm Legal and the CSRC. Such board seats cannot continue after the company goes public.
- Where the Firm member or immediate family is a material investor and no practical alternative exists to represent the investment interest. This requires approval first from the Office Manager, with advice from PD or HR, and then from the CSRC with advice from Firm Legal. No exception is available for a publicly listed company.

Factors considered for family investment board requests include:

- Whether alternatives to a board role exist.
- Whether the arrangement predates joining the Firm.
- Whether the Firm member has served or is likely to serve the company or any organization with conflicting interests.
- Whether the company's interests conflict or are likely to conflict with a client.
- Whether the company trades in public securities.
- Whether the company is or is likely to become a client.
- Whether the Firm member would control company activities.
- Whether the board position requires significant time.
- Whether the Firm member would represent the company in client interactions.
- Whether the anticipated duration of the board membership is limited.
- Whether resignation or abstention restrictions could create Firm conflicts.

Partners may join formally constituted advisory boards of for-profit companies only with prior approval. Non-Partners may not join advisory boards of for-profit companies.

An advisory board position must be approved by the Partner's Office Manager, the relevant industry leader, and the Partner's evaluator, in consultation with Firm Legal, before the Partner accepts the role.

Partners serving on advisory boards do so in their personal capacity. Any compensation must be donated. Partners must observe all Firm confidentiality obligations and may not use Firm resources for advisory board activities.

Firm Legal or Risk will provide guardrails for advisory board roles. The organization may not use the Firm's name or attribute information to COMPANY without prior written consent. The Firm member is responsible for ensuring the organization understands restrictions, including recusal obligations.

Firm members interested in joining a Public Sector Board or Advisory Board, or a Social Sector Board or Advisory Board, must seek review and approval from the SHaPE Practice. This includes hospitals, insurers, universities, industry associations, chambers of commerce, religious organizations, think tanks, and other organizations commonly incorporated as nonprofits.

Public and Social Sector board roles require SHaPE Practice approval even if the organization type is not specifically listed as disallowed.

Colleagues are not permitted to serve on boards of:

- Universities, because they purchase from and negotiate with Firm clients and may create client conflicts or the appearance of conflicts.
- Foundations, because they make grants to nonprofits the Firm may serve or where Firm colleagues may serve on boards.
- U.S. hospitals, including not-for-profit hospitals, because they compete with provider clients and purchase from or negotiate with healthcare clients.
- Non-U.S. hospitals, because they may compete for patients, staff, research funding, or have conflicting interests with health ministries or public payors.
- Industry associations, because of lobbying and advocacy activities and the possibility of conflicting interests among Firm clients.
- Mutual funds, mutual insurance companies, and insurers that engage in commercial activities.
- Public sector organizations such as government departments, regulators, and agencies, because of their lawmaking, regulatory, procurement, and commercial relationships.
- Social and political advocacy groups with unusually aggressive tactics or primary advocacy, lobbying, or legal-action purposes that may conflict with client interests or create reputational risk.

Advisory boards of public sector organizations may be selectively allowed after SHaPE approval if the role does not involve advocacy, conflict, or political controversy and also meets private sector advisory board requirements.

Firm members approved to serve on nonprofit boards may sit on board committees but cannot lobby or negotiate on behalf of the organization.

Firm members who already serve on a not-for-profit board must seek SHaPE Practice approval before renewing or extending the board service.

If an organization changes status, such as from nonprofit to for-profit or vice versa, the Firm member must consult with the SHaPE Practice and CSRC before the change to determine whether they may continue board service.

Board seats with paying clients are generally prohibited and are strictly prohibited where the organization engages in activities that could materially conflict with client interests or create reputational risk.

Board seats with organizations that are paying clients or likely to become paying clients are generally not permitted because they may appear to steer paid work to the Firm. Any decision to join or remain on a paying client board requires approval from the relevant Office Manager and industry practice leaders, with advice from Firm Legal. Higher-risk cases may be referred to the CSRC.

A paying client is an organization that paid the Firm professional fees of US$1 million in the previous two years or is anticipated to do so within the next two years.

Where a Firm member is already on a board before the Firm has an opportunity to perform paid work, the CSRC may decide that:

- The Firm declines to serve the organization or serves for reduced or no fees.
- The Firm member leaves the board.
- The Firm proceeds with paid work and the colleague remains on the board.

If the Firm performs paid work for an organization where a Firm member is on the board, the review may consider:

- Written confirmation from the client that the board member did not influence the decision to consider or retain COMPANY.
- Confirmation that there is no actual or perceived conflict or procurement-policy violation.
- Agreement that the board member will fully recuse themselves from activities directly related to the COMPANY engagement.
- Confidence that the Firm can deliver distinctive lasting impact on the topic.

If a Firm member serves on a Social or Public Sector board and the Firm provides pro bono services to the organization, the board member must recuse themselves from decisions to retain the Firm, comply with fiduciary duties, avoid sharing organization information with the client service team, and not be part of the client service team.

Municipal and regional economic development councils may have special treatment where the Firm's board service and pro bono support are core to the Firm's contribution. These roles still require prior SHaPE Practice discussion and permission.

The Accountability provisions of the Outside Activities Policy apply.
