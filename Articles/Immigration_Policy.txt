Immigration Policy

This policy applies to all colleagues globally.

The Firm and all colleagues have a legal and professional obligation to comply with immigration laws in countries where the Firm conducts business.

Before traveling internationally, colleagues must obtain confirmation from the destination location's Mobility, Immigration, and Eligibility coordinator that they have the correct visa, work permit, or other immigration documentation for the purpose and duration of travel.

For travel to the United States, colleagues must contact the Firm's United States immigration legal team.

Colleagues must never assume they have the legal right to work outside their home office or citizenship country, even for a brief period.

Colleagues must also never assume they may conduct business outside their home office or citizenship country merely because they may enter the country as a tourist, hold a visitor or business visa, or do not require a visa.

When obtaining visas, work permits, or immigration documentation for Firm-related work or business, colleagues must use only Firm-approved service providers. Colleagues may not retain personal service providers, even at their own expense.

Working outside the home office payroll country for personal reasons may be permitted only on a limited and temporary basis, subject to eligibility requirements, a maximum of 20 days in a rolling 12-month period, and prior written approval from the destination immigration team and the relevant manager or professional development manager.

Failure to comply with immigration requirements can cause:

- Disruption of client work.
- Detention.
- Deportation.
- Breach of client contract immigration obligations.
- Bans from future visas, work permits, or immigration documentation.
- Criminal prosecution.
- Fines.
- Government inspections at offices or client sites.
- Reputational damage.

Eligibility and immigration requirements must be assessed before colleagues are hired, staffed, or travel to foreign jurisdictions.

Checking immigration requirements with team members, non-immigration colleagues, travel providers, or external service providers is not sufficient. Immigration requirements vary based on nationality, tenure, academic qualifications, role, purpose of travel, and other factors.

Visa and work permit fees for Firm-related work or business are reimbursable. Corporate cards should be used where available and expenses submitted through the expense system.

Petty cash may not be used for visa and work permit fees or expenses. Expenses resulting from violating a visa requirement are personal and not reimbursable.

Mobility, Immigration, and Eligibility coordinators are the first point of contact for immigration queries. For travel to the United States, the United States immigration legal team is the first point of contact.

Citizens of countries with free travel and work rights between member nations may not need to contact immigration for travel to another member country. If unclear, the colleague should contact Mobility, Immigration, and Eligibility.

Travel teams and external travel service providers are not qualified to determine the type of work permit or visa required. That determination must be made by the destination immigration coordinator or the United States immigration legal team.

Talent Attraction must verify with immigration before extending an employment offer that a prospective hire can legally work in the relevant country. It is not sufficient to rely on recruit statements or similar cases.

Before a transfer, the sending office immigration coordinator must verify with the receiving office immigration coordinator that the move is legally permissible. If the sending office is not the colleague's citizenship country, the effect on current immigration status must also be verified.

Firm Legal can assist with Firm-related immigration matters or direct colleagues to the appropriate resource. Firm Legal does not provide advice on personal or non-Firm immigration matters.

Firm members must comply with this policy, act with integrity, and report violations. If local law is stricter, Firm members must follow local law.

The Firm does not tolerate retaliation for good-faith reporting or participation in a review or investigation.

Failure to comply may result in disciplinary action, including separation from the Firm.
