Standard on Research Collaborations

This standard supplements the External Communications Policy and applies to potential research collaborations with external organizations.

Partners must assess potential research collaborations before making commitments to research partners. The assessment should address three questions:

- Should the initiative be treated as a research collaboration?
- Does the initiative meet safeguards for collaborating institutions, research topics, and operational considerations?
- Whether and how should the research be co-branded?

The Firm distinguishes between:

- Firm research, which is initiated, scoped, and led solely by the Firm.
- Research collaborations, which are jointly scoped and carried out with one or more external organizations.
- Client work or client-commissioned research, where the scope or deliverables are driven by the client.

Research collaborations may involve:

- Non-profits, NGOs, academic institutions, or broad-based coalitions.
- Premium media organizations.
- Multilateral institutions.
- Single governments.
- Industry associations or single-issue coalitions.
- Private sector companies.

All research collaborations must meet safeguards related to the institution, topic, and operational considerations.

Collaborating institutions should:

- Be objectively regarded as leading organizations in their field.
- Be acknowledged for leading-edge thinking or shaping the future of the topic or sector.
- Have distinctive data, insights, or access that materially improve the research.
- Be organizations with which the Firm is comfortable being publicly associated.
- Focus primarily on research, convening, or services rather than advocacy or lobbying when the collaborator is an NGO or industry association.

The Firm generally prefers collaborations with more than one organization, especially when the research involves contested markets or areas with clear winners and losers.

Research topics should:

- Be a knowledge priority for a cell and endorsed by cell leadership.
- Have broad relevance, support, and demand beyond the collaborator's client service team.
- Benefit a broad range of stakeholders beyond the collaborator.
- Be jointly scoped so the work reflects the Firm's research interests as well as the partner's.
- Avoid advocacy on public policy and lobbying governments.
- Receive higher scrutiny when the topic is subject to sensitive public debate.

Co-branding decisions require care because the Firm's brand represents its reputation for excellence and independence.

The Firm generally does not co-brand with individual private sector companies. Instead, separate single-branded reports may acknowledge the collaboration. Selected exceptions may be approved for firms in which the Firm has a strategic minority investment.

The Firm rarely co-brands with a commercial entity alongside nonprofit or educational institutions. Rare cases may include commercial entities with well-established research units or a minority role in a multi-party collaboration with academic institutions or foundations.

Acknowledgements and references to the Firm require case-by-case guidance and approval from Firm Communications. Acknowledgement language should make clear that research is independent and not commissioned by a business, government, or other institution when that is applicable.

References to the Firm in client work require approval under the applicable use-of-name requirements. Where approved, language should clarify the Firm's contribution and the authors' responsibility for conclusions and recommendations.

Research collaboration with industry associations requires a high bar. The Firm should weigh relationship and reputation benefits against risks to independence, objectivity, and public perception.

Potential work with industry associations should be assessed for:

- Significant potential to strengthen client relationships, industry impact, or the Firm's reputation.
- Minimal reputational or relationship risk.
- Risk that the Firm's name may be used for credibility.
- Risk of involvement in political debates.
- A scope that produces fact-based, neutral analysis without public-policy recommendations.
- Firm Communications approval for any public references to the Firm.
- Precedent created for other industry associations or clients.

When assessing an industry association, teams should consider:

- The association's focus and reputation.
- Whether it is known for research and convening rather than lobbying.
- The nature of public debate around the topic.
- Potential media, lawmaker, or public scrutiny.
- Risk of unplanned or unapproved use of the Firm's name.
- Whether the benefits could be achieved through private interactions instead of public use of the Firm's name.

Review by a partner outside the client service team is recommended when public scrutiny can reasonably be expected.

Research or work with industry associations requires approval from the relevant office manager, regional practice leader, regional leader, and Client Service Risk Committee, with advice from cell and Firm Communications.

Firm members must comply with accountability requirements under the External Communications Policy.
